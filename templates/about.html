Motivation

The digital age has ushered in an era of unprecedented data availability across diverse domains, including agriculture, climate studies, ecology, and healthcare. This wealth of information, available in various formats such as tabular data, images, audio, and text, serves as the foundation for training advanced artificial intelligence (AI) models. However, the effective utilization of this data requires thorough preprocessing and careful selection of relevant samples. Consequently, there is a growing need for tools that enable users to visualize and comprehend data distributions effectively.

While human activities have often been associated with negative impacts on the natural world, modern technology also offers significant potential for environmental understanding and conservation. The advent of open databases allows scientists globally to share, access, and analyze data collaboratively. Nevertheless, deriving meaningful insights from this data remains a crucial challenge.

This project aims to develop a visualization tool that will enable users to explore and interpret bird sightings data across selected United States ecological centers. We will utilize datasets from the Macaulay Library and the National Science Foundation's National Ecological Observatory Network (NEON) for our research. The proposed tool will feature an interface where users can gain insights into the species of birds present in specific regions, their identification methods, and general behavior through maps and associated graphics that efficiently visualize data distributions.

Datasets

Macaulay Library

The Macaulay Library, hosted by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology, is one of the world's largest and most comprehensive repositories of wildlife media. It contains:

  • Nearly 2.4 million sound recordings of birds
  • Extensive collection of bird images and videos
  • Detailed information on bird sightings
  • Metadata associated with each unique bird species

NEON Breeding Birds Dataset

The National Ecological Observatory Network (NEON) provides a standardized, long-term dataset on breeding landbirds across its network of ecological centers. This dataset includes:

  • Bird count information collected by experienced observers
  • Data from 5-15 sampling grids at most NEON field sites
  • Observations made during the peak breeding season for resident birds
  • Records of species, sex, and distance from observation points for each bird sighted or heard

Key features of the NEON data collection methodology include:

  • Six-minute point counts following a two-minute settling period
  • Early morning sampling when birds are most active
  • Sampling locations distributed across dominant habitat classes at each site
